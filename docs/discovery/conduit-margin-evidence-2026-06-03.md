## Section 6: Honest reading of the data

This data does **not** prove all contractors are gouging. Some markup is legitimate:

- Contractors front capital for materials, which carries cost and risk
- Procurement time has real value — sourcing, ordering, coordinating delivery
- Contractors deliver to site, often absorbing fuel and truck costs
- Warranty against material failure typically sits with the contractor
- Returns and defects are handled by the contractor, not the customer

A 1.5–2× markup on materials with those services included is reasonable industry practice. The sand markup (1.6×) reflects this — delivery included, normal range.

The 4.9× on mortar and 3.7× on cinder blocks are at the high end of typical ranges. They might be:

1. Honest pricing for procurement convenience and capital fronting
2. Margin extraction where value delivered doesn't justify the markup
3. Standard pricing in a market where customers don't price-shop commodity items

A platform like Conduit cannot determine which is true from price data alone. What it can do:

1. Show customers the typical markup range for each material category in their area
2. Flag line items at the high end of the normal range (not accusation — context)
3. Explain what the markup typically buys so customers can evaluate whether they received that value
4. Help honest contractors defend their pricing with market data
5. Surface where customers can meaningfully save by self-supplying or choosing alternative operators

The goal is not to remove contractor margins. It's to make the pricing legible.

## Section 7: Juan's pricing as separate evidence

Juan charged $1,400 for roughly 3–5 days of skilled masonry work (site prep, breakdown, limestone planter construction, retaining wall). That's low relative to what this scope would bill at typical masonry market rates.

Juan may be:
- Under-pricing himself relative to market
- Operating with very low overhead that enables lower rates
- Building a customer base through competitive pricing
- Not running his business with full unit economics visibility

This is the other side of Conduit's margin intelligence value proposition. Helping small operators understand their own unit economics is as important as helping customers identify overpricing.

Juan could potentially bill $1,800–$2,100 for this scope and remain competitive in the Liberty Hill market. The gap between what he charged and what the market would bear represents unrealized revenue — his business is underselling itself.

A platform that shows small operators local market rate comparisons and their own cost structure creates value on both sides of the transaction.

## Section 8: What this proves for Conduit's value proposition

**Concrete evidence for four claims:**

**1. Customers can't see what they're paying for in bundled quotes.**  
$4,651 reads as one number. The 4.9× mortar markup is completely invisible. Customers evaluating "does this feel right?" have no access to the line-item reality.

**2. Pricing inefficiency is real and quantifiable — not theoretical.**  
$2,629 in savings, documented in receipts, on a straightforward residential masonry project. Not an edge case.

**3. Small operators undersell themselves systematically.**  
$1,400 for skilled masonry work is competitive pricing that likely doesn't reflect full market value. The operator doesn't know what the market would bear — or doesn't have the credibility collateral to charge it.

**4. The knowledge gap is worth ~57% of the invoice total.**  
Customers with material cost access and the willingness to unbundle saved more than half the quoted cost. The knowledge asymmetry is priced into every bundled quote.

## Section 10: Limitations of single data point

One project. One contractor quote. One alternative contractor. One regional market. This does not prove industry-wide patterns.

To validate the Conduit margin intelligence thesis with confidence:

- 5–10 more documented comparisons across different trades (HVAC, electrical, roofing, plumbing, concrete/flatwork)
- Data from multiple regional markets — Liberty Hill results may not generalize
- Supplier price data to build "typical markup range" reference tables (this is the hard problem)
- Customer interviews about how they currently evaluate quotes and where they feel uncertainty
- Contractor interviews about their own pricing visibility and competitive pressure

**This document is the first data point, not the conclusion.** The thesis is supported; the evidence is not yet sufficient to build from with high confidence.
